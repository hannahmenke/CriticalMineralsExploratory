# White Paper Outline: The Digital Rocks Community and the Future of UK/European Critical Minerals

**Working Title:** Unlocking the Microscale: How Digital Rock Physics Can Secure Supply Chains for UK and European Critical Minerals
**Target Audience:** Policymakers (UK Gov, EU Commission), Mining Executives, Research Funding Bodies (UKRI, Horizon Europe), and Geoscience/Materials Researchers.

---

## 1. Executive Summary
*   **The Problem:** UK and Europe face acute supply chain vulnerabilities for Critical Raw Materials (CRMs) essential for the energy transition and defense, addressed by the UK Vision 2035 and EU Critical Raw Materials Act (CRMA).
*   **The Solution:** The "Digital Rocks" or Digital Rock Physics (DRP) community—historically anchored in oil and gas—possesses world-class expertise in 3D X-ray micro-computed tomography (micro-CT), multi-scale imaging, and pore-scale fluid simulation.
*   **The Ask:** A coordinated pivot of this expertise towards complex ore characterization, processing efficiency, and mine waste valorization.
*   **Key Takeaways:** DRP shortens evaluation times, lowers environmental impact, maximizes yields from low-grade ores, and reclassifies mine tailings as strategic reserves.

## 2. Context: The Geopolitical Imperative and the Global Race
*   **The Supply Squeeze & Investment Paradox:** Skyrocketing demand for lithium (up nearly 30% in 2024), cobalt, REEs, and graphite. Yet, as the IEA Global Critical Minerals Outlook highlights, global mining exploration activity has plateaued, and investment growth has slowed. Efficiency and yield optimization from existing deposits are now more critical than ever.
*   **Market Concentration:** The IEA reports that the top three refining nations now control 86% of the market for key energy minerals.
*   **EU Critical Raw Materials Act (CRMA):** 2030 targets (10% extraction, 40% processing, 25% recycling) designed directly to combat this hyper-concentration.
*   **UK "Vision 2035":** Focus on domestic capability, midstream processing, circular economy, and leveraging academic strengths.
*   **The Global Benchmark (US DOE):** The US is aggressively funding advanced characterization through programs like the ARPA-E ROCKS initiative and the $80M "Mine of the Future" grants. The UK/EU must mobilize its DRP community to maintain transatlantic competitiveness and foster collaboration.
*   **The Shift in Mining:** Ore grades are dropping globally. "Easy" deposits are gone. The new frontier requires extreme precision.

## 3. The Digital Rocks Paradigm
*   **What is Digital Rock Physics?** Non-destructive 3D imaging (micro-CT), coupled with advanced image processing, AI/machine learning segmentation, and numerical simulations.
*   **The Pivot from Hydrocarbons to Minerals:** How existing infrastructure (synchrotrons, lab-scale scanners, supercomputing clusters) and software workflows can be directly applied to hard-rock mining and mineralogy.
*   **Data Federation:** Taking inspiration from the US DOE NETL's "Digital Rocks Portal", there is a critical need to establish a federated European "Digital Ore Database" to share tomographic data of critical mineral deposits.

## 4. Pillar I: Exploration and Advanced Characterization
*   **Fueling AI-Based Exploration:** The IEA explicitly calls out AI-driven exploration as a critical 2025 innovation. DRP provides the ultra-high-resolution, 3D ground-truth datasets required to train these predictive geological models.
*   **Rapid Orebody Assessment:** Aligning with the goals of programs like ARPA-E ROCKS to drastically shorten the timeline from discovery to production by using cutting-edge sensing and analytical methods.
*   **3D Ore Characterization:** Moving beyond 2D thin sections to fully quantify 3D mineral associations, grain sizes, and liberation characteristics in complex, low-grade deposits.
*   **"Invisible" Minerals:** Using advanced contrast techniques and correlative microscopy to find finely disseminated critical minerals (e.g., REEs in clays, lithium in pegmatites).
*   **Predictive Geometallurgy:** Building 3D digital twins of rock formations to predict how ores will behave during crushing and chemical extraction before physical mining begins.

## 5. Pillar II: Optimizing Mineral Processing and Extraction
*   **Comminution (Crushing & Grinding):** Mining uses ~6% of all global electricity, mostly for crushing rocks. DRP micro-scale fracture dynamics studies can optimize breakage, saving massive amounts of energy and reducing carbon footprint.
*   **In-Situ Leaching and Heap Leaching:** Simulating pore-scale fluid flow to understand lixivium (leaching fluid) transport. Optimizing permeability and dissolution rates for critical minerals without moving millions of tons of rock.
*   **Direct Extraction Technologies:** The critical role of engineered porous materials (such as MOFs, zeolites, and microporous polymers) in Direct Lithium Extraction (DLE) and Rare Earth Element (REE) separation from leachates. These materials act as highly selective ion sieves, drastically reducing the environmental footprint compared to traditional evaporative ponds or bulk acid leaching.
*   **Real-Time Processing:** Integrating rapid micro-CT scans into the process loop (like the EU's "Real-Time Mining" project) to adjust metallurgical circuits continuously.

## 6. Pillar III: Tailings Valorization and the Circular Economy
*   **Finding Treasure in Trash:** Legacy mine tailings often contain significant amounts of currently critical minerals. The IEA Outlook highlights the "re-mining of tailings" as a prime innovation area to bridge supply deficits.
*   **Calibrating Advanced Sorting:** IEA points to hyperspectral imaging and AI sorting for recycling; micro-CT acts as the fundamental calibration tool for these macro-scale industrial sensors.
*   **Secondary Feedstocks (The CORE-CM Approach):** Mirroring the US DOE's CORE-CM initiative, applying micro-CT to characterize unconventional feedstocks like coal refuse, fly ash, and acid mine drainage across Europe's industrial heartlands.
*   **Digital Inventories:** Synergizing with efforts (like B.C.'s digital compilations) to 3D-characterize and inventory waste rock at abandoned or active sites in the UK (e.g., Cornwall) and EU.
*   **Recycling End-of-Life Tech:** Applying micro-CT to accurately characterize e-waste and battery black mass to optimize mechanical and chemical recycling pathways.

## 7. Next-Generation Digital Rocks: Expanding the Horizon
*   **Integrating Chemistry with Structure:** Digital rocks must evolve beyond purely physical characterization (porosity, permeability). The field needs to develop multi-modal workflows to fuse 3D structural data (micro-CT) with chemical mapping (e.g., 3D micro-XRF, EDS, hyperspectral imaging) to accurately track elemental speciation and reactive transport during complex extraction processes.
*   **The Shift to Synthetic Porous Materials:** Transitioning the DRP toolkit from natural geology to engineered materials. Applying these techniques to design, characterize, and optimize synthetic porous media—such as MOFs, zeolites, ion-imprinted membranes, and battery electrodes—that are actively revolutionizing both the extraction of critical minerals (e.g., DLE) and their end-use in clean energy.
*   **Regulating Energy Transfer Vectors:** Building on the recent framework established in *Science* (Farber, Rücker, et al., 2025), DRP techniques must be used to holistically design porous structures across all length scales (from angstroms to centimeters) to precisely regulate the flow of mass, charge, heat, pressure, and radiation to maximize surface-area-to-volume efficiency.

## 8. Environmental Impact and Sustainability
*   **Carbon storage (CCUS):** Leveraging DRP to ensure safe mineralisation of CO2 in mafic/ultramafic rocks alongside extraction.
*   **Reduced Water Usage:** Optimizing floatation and leaching circuits through better fundamental understanding of particle surface areas.

## 9. The Road Ahead: Challenges and Recommendations
*   **Standardization:** The need for standardized imaging protocols and machine learning models across the European community.
*   **Upscaling:** Bridging the gap from micro-CT scale (microns/mm) to core scale to mine-site scale.
*   **Policy Recommendations:** 
    *   Targeted UK/EU grants (Horizon Europe, UKRI) specifically matching DRP academics with mining juniors and chemical engineers.
    *   Incentives for "digital twin" required reporting on new CRM projects.
    *   Creating a centralized UK/European "Digital Ore Database."

## 10. Conclusion
*   Reiteration that leveraging the existing, highly advanced DRP community provides the UK and Europe with a unique technological advantage in the global race for secure, sustainable critical minerals.
