\title{\textbf{Unlocking the Microscale: How Digital Rock Physics Can Secure Supply Chains for UK and European Critical Minerals}}
\author{Hannah P. Menke \and Maja Rücker}
\date{\today}

\begin{abstract}
The United Kingdom and Europe face acute supply chain vulnerabilities for Critical Raw Materials (CRMs) essential for the energy transition and defense. While policy frameworks such as the UK Vision 2035 and the EU Critical Raw Materials Act (CRMA) set aggressive targets for domestic extraction, processing, and recycling, the technical means to achieve extreme efficiency from increasingly complex, low-grade ores lag behind. The ``Digital Rocks'' or Digital Rock Physics (DRP) community—historically anchored in the oil and gas sector—possesses world-class expertise in multi-scale, multi-modal imaging (including CT, nano-CT, MRI, PET, and FIB-SEM) and pore-scale fluid simulation. This white paper proposes a coordinated pivot of this expertise towards complex ore characterization, specific extraction technologies like Direct Lithium Extraction (DLE), and mine waste valorization. By employing DRP to shorten evaluation times, lower environmental impacts, and design the next generation of synthetic porous materials, the UK and Europe can establish a unique technological advantage in the global race for secure, sustainable critical minerals.
\end{abstract}

\section{Context: The Geopolitical Imperative and the Global Race}
The global transition to clean energy technologies has triggered skyrocketing demand for critical minerals such as lithium, cobalt, rare earth elements (REEs), and graphite. For instance, global lithium demand surged by nearly 30\% in 2024 alone \cite{iea_outlook_2024}. Yet, this demand increase coincides with a supply squeeze and an investment paradox: as highlighted by the International Energy Agency's (IEA) Global Critical Minerals Outlook, global mining exploration activity has plateaued, and investment growth has slowed \cite{iea_outlook_2025}. Consequently, achieving extreme efficiency and yield optimization from existing deposits is now more critical than ever.

Compounding this issue is the hyper-concentration of the market. The IEA reports that the top three refining nations now control 86\% of the market for key energy minerals \cite{iea_outlook_2024}. In response, the EU Critical Raw Materials Act (CRMA) established 2030 targets requiring 10\% extraction, 40\% processing, and 25\% recycling to occur domestically \cite{eu_crma}. Concurrently, the UK's ``Vision 2035'' focuses heavily on midstream processing, circular economy principles, and leveraging world-leading academic strengths \cite{uk_vision_2035}.

Globally, the United States is aggressively funding advanced characterization through programs like the Department of Energy’s ARPA-E ROCKS initiative—aimed at rapid, high-resolution ore assessment—and the \$80M ``Mine of the Future'' grants \cite{doe_arpae}. The UK and Europe must mobilize their existing DRP communities to maintain transatlantic competitiveness and foster effective collaboration. As ore grades drop globally—with average copper grades falling approximately 40\% since 1991 \cite{calvo_copper_2016}—the ``easy'' deposits have vanished, meaning the new frontier of mining requires unprecedented micro-scale precision.

\section{The Digital Rocks Paradigm}
Digital Rock Physics (DRP) utilizes a comprehensive suite of 3D and 4D imaging technologies to systematically map the structure of any porous material. While often associated primarily with X-ray micro-computed tomography ($\mu$CT), DRP actually spans a much broader array of multi-modal techniques. This includes medical and industrial CT, nano-CT, Positron Emission Tomography (PET), Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI), Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR), Focused Ion Beam Scanning Electron Microscopy (FIB-SEM), and Helium Ion Microscopy (HIM). Coupling these diverse imaging methods with advanced image processing, AI-driven segmentation, and numerical simulations creates a unified platform for tracking mass, charge, and fluid flow \cite{drp_blunt_2013}.

Historically, this toolkit was developed to optimize hydrocarbon extraction. However, as the energy transition accelerates, there is an urgent need to pivot this existing infrastructure—including synchrotrons, lab-scale scanners, and supercomputing clusters—towards hard-rock mining and mineralogy. 

Taking inspiration from the US DOE National Energy Technology Laboratory’s ``Digital Rocks Portal,'' there is a critical need to establish a federated European ``Digital Ore Database'' to share multi-modal tomographic data of critical mineral deposits, thereby democratizing access to high-resolution structural data \cite{netl_digitalrocks, prodanovic_digitalrocks_2015}.

\section{Pillar I: Exploration and Advanced Characterization}
The implementation of DRP fundamentally shifts mineral exploration strategies, particularly by fueling AI-based exploration. The IEA explicitly defines AI-driven exploration as a critical innovation for 2025 and beyond, specifically in predicting hidden anomalies \cite{iea_outlook_2025, ai_mineral_prospectivity_2022}. DRP provides the ultra-high-resolution, 3D ground-truth datasets that are absolutely required to reliably train these predictive geological models. Furthermore, aligning with goals analogous to the US DOE's ARPA-E ROCKS program, DRP drastically shortens the timeline from initial discovery to commercial production using cutting-edge sensing and analytical methods, enabling rapid orebody assessment \cite{doe_arpae}.

Moving beyond traditional 2D thin sections alone, multi-modal tomographic approaches (ranging from macro-CT down to nano-CT) fully quantify 3D mineral associations, grain sizes, and liberation characteristics. When unified with 2D chemical maps via correlative techniques, researchers can locate finely disseminated ``invisible'' critical minerals, such as rare earth elements in clays or lithium in pegmatites, that would otherwise escape detection \cite{microct_mining_2023}. Ultimately, by building 3D digital twins of rock formations, metallurgists are empowered with predictive geometallurgy. This allows them to simulate and predict exactly how ores will behave during crushing and chemical extraction processes prior to physical mining, optimizing capital-intensive flowsheets from day one \cite{lishchuk_predictive_2020}.

\section{Pillar II: Optimizing Mineral Processing and Extraction}
The extraction of critical minerals is notoriously energy- and water-intensive, yet DRP offers robust pathways to optimize these core processes. For example, comminution (crushing and grinding) is an exceptionally energy-intensive step, accounting for up to 4\% of total global electricity consumption and often exceeding 50\% of a single mine site's power demand \cite{norgate_comminution_2006, ceec_comminution_2023}. DRP enables rigorous micro-scale fracture dynamics studies to optimize breakage patterns, saving massive amounts of energy and drastically reducing the sector's carbon footprint \cite{microct_fracture_comminution_2021}. 

Similarly, in operations such as in-situ and heap leaching, engineers can utilize dynamic imaging (e.g., PET or MRI) and simulate pore-scale fluid flow to safely map and understand the transport of lixivium (leaching fluid) \cite{mri_heap_leach_2021}. This deep operational understanding optimizes permeability and dissolution rates for critical minerals, circumventing the need to physically excavate millions of tons of waste rock \cite{leaching_pores_2024}. Furthermore, DRP is essential in characterizing the complex matrices involved in emerging Direct Extraction Technologies. Engineered porous materials—such as Metal-Organic Frameworks (MOFs), zeolites, and microporous polymers like AquaPIMs—play a critical role in Direct Lithium Extraction (DLE) and REE separation from leachates \cite{mofs_lithium_2024}. Acting as highly selective ion sieves, these materials drastically reduce the environmental footprint compared to traditional evaporative ponds or bulk acid leaching \cite{dle_technology_2023}. Finally, integrating rapid multi-modal scans directly into the operational process loop—similar to the EU's ``Real-Time Mining'' project—allows metallurgical circuits to be continuously adjusted in real-time based on high-fidelity structural data \cite{eu_realtime_mining}.

\section{Pillar III: Tailings Valorization and the Circular Economy}
Developing a circular economy is a cornerstone of both UK and EU critical minerals strategies, particularly regarding the concept of finding treasure in trash. Legacy mine tailings frequently contain significant, unrecovered amounts of critical minerals such as tellurium, antimony, and tungsten. Indeed, the IEA Outlook highlights the ``re-mining of tailings'' as a primary innovation area necessary to bridge looming supply deficits \cite{iea_outlook_2025}, and targeted valorization of these sites requires vast structural characterization \cite{tailings_valorization_2022}. 

To effectively recycle these materials, advanced sorting mechanisms must be employed and precisely calibrated. While the IEA points to hyperspectral imaging and AI sorting for macro-scale recycling \cite{iea_outlook_2024}, high-resolution 3D imaging acts as the fundamental micro-scale calibration tool for these robust industrial sensors. Mirroring the US DOE's CORE-CM initiative, multi-scale DRP methods can be broadly applied to characterize unconventional secondary feedstocks—like coal refuse, fly ash, and acid mine drainage—across Europe's industrial heartlands \cite{doe_corecm}. Synergizing with digital compilation efforts, DRP can 3D-characterize and inventory historic waste rock \cite{tailings_digital_2024}. Furthermore, advanced techniques like FIB-SEM and nano-CT accurately characterize e-waste and complex battery ``black mass'' at the finest sub-micron scales to optimize both mechanical and chemical recycling pathways by mapping structural degradation and elemental composition \cite{battery_blackmass_2023}.

\section{Next-Generation Digital Rocks: Expanding the Horizon}
As the field of Digital Rock Physics matures, it must expand its capabilities to address increasingly complex chemical and synthetic challenges. Foremost among these is the integration of chemistry with structure via correlative microscopy \cite{correlative_microscopy_2021}. Digital rocks must evolve beyond purely physical characterization metrics, such as porosity and permeability, to provide explicit chemical context directly to 3D structures. The field must actively adopt multi-modal correlative workflows that seamlessly register 2D, high-resolution elemental data from thin sections—utilizing advanced Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM) paired with ultra-fast Backscattered Electron and X-ray (BEX) detectors—directly onto 3D multi-modal structural volumes. This chemical painting of 3D structures is absolutely essential to accurately track elemental speciation and reactive transport during complex, multi-phase extraction processes.

Simultaneously, we must navigate the shift to synthetic porous materials, transitioning the DRP toolkit from natural geology to engineered materials. Applying these precise imaging and simulation techniques to design, characterize, and optimize synthetic porous media—such as MOFs, zeolites, ion-imprinted membranes, and battery electrodes—is actively revolutionizing both the extraction of critical minerals via DLE and their ultimate end-use in clean energy applications. Building on recent frameworks established in the literature, DRP techniques must be used to holistically design these porous structures across all length scales, ranging from angstroms to centimeters, in order to optimally regulate the flow of mass, charge, heat, pressure, and radiation \cite{farber_science_2025}. Utilizing tools like NMR to probe sub-nanometer pores alongside macroscopic CT allows for the precise optimization of surface-area-to-volume efficiency in entirely synthetic domains.

\section{The AI Revolution in Digital Rocks: Surrogates and Upscaling}
The integration of Machine Learning (ML) is fundamentally transforming Digital Rock Physics by directly addressing its historical bottleneck: the extreme computational cost of pore-scale simulations. Traditional DRP simulations—particularly those modeling multi-phase reactive transport—often require prohibitive supercomputing resources. Today, ML surrogate models built on deep neural networks act as lightning-fast proxies for these complex physics simulations \cite{ml_surrogate_poreflow_2020}. By learning from existing high-fidelity fluid flow data, surrogate models can predict porosity, permeability, and reactive behavior in entirely new rock volumes in fractions of a second, enabling real-time process control and massive parameter sweeps \cite{karniadakis_pinns_2021}.

Furthermore, Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) are revolutionizing the reliability of these models. Unlike standard data-driven AI, PINNs natively embed physical laws (such as Navier-Stokes equations for fluid flow) directly into their loss functions. This mechanism ensures that the AI's predictions strictly adhere to the fundamental laws of physics, allowing researchers to accurately infer fluid dynamics even when tomographic data is sparse or noisy \cite{raissi_pinns_2019}.

Crucially, ML provides the ultimate solution to the DRP ``upscaling'' challenge. Because high-resolution imaging (like nano-CT) is confined to microscopic sample volumes, translating those insights to the macroscopic mine-site scale has always been difficult. Modern ML workflows solve this by quantitatively identifying representative microscopic texture patterns across massive datasets, running targeted physics simulations solely on exactly those key textures, and aggressively upscaling the resulting physical properties to the core and reservoir scale \cite{ml_upscaling_drp_2022, menke_machine_learning_2025}. This seamless integration of AI and physics transforms DRP from a microscopic analytical tool into a macro-scale predictive powerhouse.

\section{Environmental Impact and Sustainability}
The deployment of DRP also carries profound implications for environmental sustainability. Regarding carbon storage (CCUS), leveraging DRP ensures the safe and rapid mineralisation of $CO_2$ inside mafic and ultramafic rocks. Building on successes like the CarbFix project in Iceland \cite{gislason_carbfix_2018}, performing CCUS alongside extraction operations provides a dual benefit: securing vital domestic resources while achieving permanent carbon sequestration and providing crucial insights into dynamic subsurface gas storage \cite{kelemen_peridotite_2020, menke_hydrogen_2024}. Furthermore, by gaining a better fundamental understanding of particle surface areas, wettability, and multi-phase flow via fast X-ray imaging \cite{rucker_fast_xray_2017}, traditional floatation and leaching circuits can be significantly optimized, leading to dramatically reduced fresh water consumption across the mining sector.

\section{The Road Ahead: Challenges and Recommendations}
To fully realize the potential of DRP in securing critical minerals, several hurdles must be addressed. There is an urgent need for standardization, particularly concerning imaging protocols and machine learning models across the European research and industrial community. Additionally, significant technical challenges remain in upscaling, specifically in bridging the wide gap from nano-scale structural data (FIB-SEM/nano-CT) and micro-scale up to the core scale (Macro-CT/MRI/PET), and ultimately mapping that granular data to the massive mine-site scale.

To overcome these challenges, active policy recommendations are required:

\begin{tcolorbox}[enhanced, colback=gray!3!white, colframe=blue!40!black, title=\Large\textbf{Key Policy Recommendations}, colbacktitle=blue!40!black, drop fuzzy shadow, fonttitle=\bfseries, breakable, arc=2mm, boxrule=1pt]
\textbf{1. Bridge the Commercialization ``Valley of Death'':}\\
Move beyond basic research grants. UKRI and Horizon Europe must establish dedicated funding vehicles that mandate tripartite partnerships: DRP academic institutions, junior exploration companies, and chemical engineering scale-up facilities. This directly de-risks the translation of micro-scale insights into commercial-scale pilot plants.

\vspace{2mm}
\textbf{2. Mandate ``Digital Ore Passports'':}\\
To access fast-tracked permitting under the EU CRMA and UK initiatives, new mining and recycling projects should be heavily incentivized to submit standardized, multi-modal tomographic and chemical datasets. This ``Digital Ore Passport'' ensures operational transparency and enforces maximum yield extraction.

\vspace{2mm}
\textbf{3. Establish a Federated Digital Ore Database:}\\
Stop siloing proprietary data. Launch a publicly funded, open-access ``European Digital Ore Database,'' mirroring the US DOE's initiatives. This federated repository will democratize high-resolution structural datasets, enabling AI developers globally to train the next generation of predictive geometallurgy surrogate models.

\vspace{2mm}
\textbf{4. Fund Cross-Disciplinary Training Centres:}\\
The future of critical minerals requires a hybridized workforce. Establish specific Centres for Doctoral Training (CDTs) focused entirely on the intersection of multi-modal digital physics, machine learning, and extractive metallurgy, ensuring the UK and Europe retain the brightest minds driving the energy transition.
\end{tcolorbox}

\section{Conclusion}
Leveraging the existing, highly advanced Digital Rock Physics community provides the UK and Europe with a unique technological advantage in the global race for secure and sustainable critical minerals. By transitioning this expertise from hydrocarbons to hard rocks and orchestrating a vast array of imaging modalities to characterize engineered porous materials, we can achieve the extreme efficiencies required to meet the ambitious targets set forth by current geopolitical strategies, ensuring a resilient supply chain for the energy transition.
